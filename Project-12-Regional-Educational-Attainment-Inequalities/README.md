# Project 12: Regional Educational Attainment Inequalities by Disability Status in England

## Introduction

Educational attainment is an important social determinant of health and is associated with employment opportunities, income, and long-term wellbeing. Educational outcomes may vary across geographical regions and may also differ between disabled and non-disabled populations.

This project explores regional differences in degree-level educational attainment among adults aged 21–64 years in England using Office for National Statistics (ONS) data.

---

## Data Source

Office for National Statistics (ONS)

Disability and Education Reference Tables

Table 7: Highest level of qualification of people aged 21 to 64 by disability status and English region.

England, year ending June 2019 to year ending June 2021.

---

## Research Question

How does degree-level educational attainment vary among disabled and non-disabled adults across English regions?

---

## Methods

* Imported ONS regional educational attainment data into R.
* Filtered the dataset to Degree or Equivalent qualifications.
* Selected the most recent available estimates (2020/21).
* Compared attainment levels across English regions.
* Calculated disability-related attainment gaps for each region.

---

## Visualisations

### Figure 1: Degree-Level Educational Attainment by Region

A grouped bar chart comparing degree-level attainment among disabled and non-disabled adults across English regions.

### Figure 2: Disability-Related Educational Attainment Gap by Region

A ranked bar chart showing the difference in degree-level attainment between non-disabled and disabled adults across regions.

---

## Results

### Figure 1

Degree-level educational attainment varied substantially across regions. Non-disabled adults consistently reported higher attainment than disabled adults in every region.

London reported the highest levels of degree attainment for both groups.

### Figure 2

Educational attainment gaps varied across England.

London exhibited the largest disability-related attainment gap (25 percentage points), while regions such as the South West showed smaller gaps.

---

## Key Findings

* Educational attainment varied across English regions.
* Non-disabled adults consistently reported higher degree-level attainment than disabled adults.
* London had the highest degree-level attainment overall.
* Educational attainment gaps differed between regions.
* London demonstrated the largest disability-related educational attainment gap.

---

## Public Health Relevance

Educational attainment is closely linked to health, employment opportunities, and socioeconomic wellbeing.

Regional differences in educational outcomes may contribute to wider health inequalities across England. Understanding where educational inequalities are greatest can help inform targeted interventions and policy development.

---

## Limitations

* The analysis is descriptive and does not establish causality.
* Only degree-level attainment was examined.
* Results are based on aggregated ONS data.
* Other factors such as age, sex, ethnicity, and impairment severity were not explored.

---

## Conclusion

Educational attainment varied across English regions and remained consistently higher among non-disabled adults. Although London reported the highest levels of degree attainment overall, it also demonstrated the largest disability-related attainment gap, highlighting important regional inequalities in educational outcomes.
